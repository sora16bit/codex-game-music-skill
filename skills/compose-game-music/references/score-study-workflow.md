# Score Study Workflow

Use this to learn from existing sheet music, MIDI, MusicXML, ABC notation, public-domain works, licensed/user-provided scores, official licensed game-music folios owned by the user, public analyses, or short reference excerpts. The goal is to extract compositional principles, not to copy protected expression.

## Source Rules

Prefer:

- Public-domain scores.
- User-provided scores that the user has rights to use.
- Official licensed game-music books or score excerpts when the user owns/provides them.
- Self-authored examples.
- Short analytical excerpts when legally appropriate.
- Abstracted note tables created for analysis.

Avoid:

- Copying a recognizable copyrighted melody, bassline, arrangement, or orchestration.
- Building a local library of copyrighted scores or audio without permission.
- Treating fan transcriptions as reusable source material.
- Producing "in the style of [living artist / specific soundtrack]" outputs.
- Reusing distinctive hooks, exact phrase contours, or signature progressions from a named work.

Allowed output from study:

- Form diagrams.
- Phrase-length patterns.
- Motif-development methods.
- Harmonic functions in Roman numerals or scale degrees.
- Rhythmic grammar.
- Texture and orchestration principles.
- Loop and cadence strategies.
- Generic scene-fit lessons.

## Study Card

For each studied piece or excerpt, create a concise card:

- Source status: public domain, user-provided, original, or short analytical excerpt.
- Rights boundary: exact-note analysis allowed, abstract-only, or do-not-use.
- Scene/use: what game situation it could support.
- Meter/pulse: felt beat, subdivision, accent grammar.
- Form: phrase count, A/B/return shape, repetition strategy.
- Motif: contour type and rhythmic fingerprint, described abstractly.
- Development: sequence, inversion, fragmentation, augmentation, diminution, displacement, reharmonization, register transfer, interruption.
- Harmony: tonal center, mode, harmonic rhythm, cadence behavior, loop-return method.
- Bass: pedal, walking, ostinato, contrary, sparse, root support, or ground.
- Counterline/inner motion: answer, contrary motion, suspension, echo, or none.
- Texture: point/line/plane roles and articulation.
- Scene evidence: why the piece implies place/action/stakes.
- Transfer rule: one reusable principle stated without copying notes.

## Reduction Pass

Before using a reference, reduce it:

1. Remove exact pitches unless the source is public domain or user-owned and exact analysis is needed.
2. Convert melody to contour: up, down, repeat, leap, step, neighbor, hold.
3. Convert harmony to functions or scale degrees.
4. Convert rhythm to durations and accent groups.
5. Convert arrangement to roles: lead, bass, counterline, plane, pulse, event.
6. Write one original design rule for the current cue.

Example transfer:

- Do not: "Use this exact four-note melody."
- Do: "Use a repeated-note knock followed by a falling third to imply a remembered domestic object."

## Composition From Study

When composing after study:

1. Pick 2-3 study cards, not 10.
2. Extract one principle from each.
3. Build an original motif using different pitches and contour.
4. Choose a harmonic plan that serves the requested scene, not the reference.
5. Use the references to check form and development, not to fill notes.
6. Run the music-first gate before rendering.

## Game Score Study Directions

When studying actual game music without user-provided licensed sheets, stay at mechanism level:

- Action platformer music: inspect how pulse, syncopation, rests, and SFX space support running/jumping.
- Adventure field music: inspect how small motifs, area contrast, and loop return create place identity.
- RPG leitmotifs: inspect how a motif survives changes in tempo, mode, harmony, register, and instrumentation.
- Combat/boss music: inspect how bass motion, harmonic rhythm, register collision, and phrase interruption create threat without relying on default snare/backbeat.
- Ambient/environmental music: inspect how sparse contour, slow harmonic motion, and texture create memory without constant noise.

Never use a studied cue as a note source. Use it as a checklist for whether the new cue has comparable function, form, and game integration.

## Public-Domain Study Directions

Useful categories:

- Haunted/domestic: short minor waltzes, old dances, simple piano character pieces.
- Forest/fairy/ruin: modal folk melodies, pastoral miniatures, open fifth drones.
- Infernal/fate: minor-key marches, chromatic sequences, pedal-point organ or choral writing.
- Snow/emptiness: slow chorales, suspended/open-fifth writing, sparse piano miniatures.
- Field travel: folk dances, modal marches, walking bass studies, simple rondo forms.

For each category, study the mechanism:

- What makes it move?
- What makes it return?
- What makes it memorable?
- What makes it belong to a place?

## Anti-Copy Check

Before final output, verify:

- The new motif is not a recognizable reference melody.
- The phrase order is not a near-clone of one reference.
- The bassline is not a copied signature line.
- The arrangement does not recreate a known recording.
- The response does not claim imitation of a named artist or soundtrack.

If any check fails, rewrite the motif, rhythm, harmony, and arrangement until only abstract principles remain.
