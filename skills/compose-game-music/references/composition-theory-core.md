# Composition Theory Core

Use this before composing or revising BGM when the cue must become a stronger composition rather than a nicer sound bed. This file assumes prior outputs may be theoretically weak. Do not hide weak writing behind timbre, ambience, noise, distortion, or extra layers.

## Required Design Card

Before writing notes, fill these fields:

- Function: what the music makes the player do or feel.
- Meter and pulse: the felt beat, subdivision, accent grouping, and whether motion is walking, floating, ritual, chase, battle, or stillness.
- Tonal center and mode: home pitch, color tones, avoid tones, and how the loop returns home.
- Motif: 3-8 notes or one rhythmic cell, with interval shape and rhythm.
- Bass function: pedal, walking bass, ostinato, ground bass, harmonic root support, contrary motion, threat pulse, or absence.
- Harmonic plan: static pedal, modal vamp, cadence cycle, chromatic pressure, suspension chain, or call-response harmony.
- Phrase form: 2, 4, 8, 16, or 32-bar structure with A/A'/B/bridge/fill/return.
- Development device: sequence, inversion, augmentation, diminution, rhythmic displacement, fragmentation, extension, reharmonization, register transfer, or interruption.
- Counterline: answer, contrary motion, inner voice, echo, antagonist line, or none by deliberate choice.
- Cadence and loop return: how the end of the loop points back to the start without feeling arbitrary.
- Sustain and voice-leading plan: which voice is held, tied, suspended, anticipated, delayed, or moved while another voice stays still.

If any required field is unknown, decide it before rendering.

## Minimum Composition Theory Pass

Before arranging or rendering, prove the cue is a composition and not a texture preset. Write a compact sketch that satisfies these minimums:

1. Motive to phrase: start from a 3-8 note/rhythm motive, then make a phrase by repeating, answering, sequencing, extending, or contrasting it. Do not generate every bar from scratch.
2. Phrase question and answer: for an 8-bar loop, bars 1-4 should feel like an opening/antecedent and bars 5-8 like an answer/consequent, return, or stronger cadence. For a 4-bar loop, use statement, answer, variation, return.
3. Voice leading over chord labels: treat every chord as several horizontal voices. Keep common tones when possible; move other voices by step or shortest practical distance; avoid making all voices jump together.
4. Dissonance behavior: use passing tones, neighbor tones, suspensions, appoggiaturas, anticipations, and pedal tones only with a job: preparation, tension, color, or resolution. If a nonchord tone never resolves or repeats without purpose, it becomes random wrongness.
5. Bass function: the bass must either walk, pedal, answer, create pressure, or prepare the loop return. Root notes alone are not a bass line.
6. Harmonic rhythm: decide when harmony changes and why. A field cue often benefits from a stable pedal plus small upper motion; action needs faster harmonic pressure.
7. Cadence and loop: the final bar must point back to bar 1 through a pickup, held dominant/color tone, bass turn, unresolved suspension, or rhythmic fill.
8. Performability check: imagine the sketch on piano with sustain pedal. If it sounds like blocked chord triggers with a melody pasted on top, rewrite voice leading, rhythm, and durations.

Use theory as diagnosis, not decoration. Naming a mode, chord, or scale does not make music. The listener hears phrase direction, tension/release, contour, repetition/variation, bass gravity, and how notes continue through time.

## Combination Over Single Technique

Never answer a composition failure with one isolated fix. "Hold notes longer" still fails if rhythm, bass, motive, harmony, inner motion, and texture do not work together.

For every cue, define the working combination:

- Motive gives identity.
- Rhythm gives physical behavior.
- Bass gives gravity and direction.
- Harmony gives color and tension/release.
- Inner voices give continuity and intelligence.
- Counterline gives dialogue or opposition.
- Texture gives density, distance, and surface.
- Timbre gives world identity.
- Loop return gives game usability.

If any layer is present only because "music usually has it", remove or rewrite it. If a technique does not interact with at least two other dimensions, it is probably a gimmick.

## Motif Rules

A motif is not any repeated note list. It needs identity:

- Give it one memorable contour: rise-fall, fall-rise, leap-resolution, neighbor turn, repeated knock, or compressed cell.
- Give it one rhythmic fingerprint: long-short, short-short-long, syncopation, dotted shape, 3+3+2, or held-answer.
- Keep the first motif simple enough to remember after one hearing.
- Repeat it with a reason: answer, intensify, distort, resolve, or relocate.
- Avoid changing pitch, rhythm, timbre, and register all at once; then the listener cannot recognize development.

Weak motif symptoms:

- Every phrase starts from scratch.
- The lead is only decorative.
- Muting percussion removes all identity.
- The track title is the only clue to the scene.

## Phrase And Form

Game loops still need phrase argument. Use one of these forms:

- 4-bar loop: statement, answer, variation, return.
- 8-bar loop: A statement, A' variation, B contrast, return/fill.
- 16-bar loop: A/A'/B/A'' with a clear midpoint change.
- 32-bar loop: two 16-bar arcs or four 8-bar scenes with shared motif.

Every phrase should do one job:

- State identity.
- Answer or contradict the prior phrase.
- Thin out to create space.
- Increase density or register.
- Reharmonize the motif.
- Prepare the loop return.

Avoid "one texture for 60 seconds with random events." If the loop has no phrase-level change, it is an ambience bed, not a composed cue.

## Harmony

Harmony should express scene function, not only key choice.

- Pedal: place, ritual, dread, stability, or heat pressure.
- Modal vamp: travel, folk/world identity, dungeon, mystery.
- Suspensions: unresolved tension, sacred/ancient places, frozen or haunted spaces.
- Chromatic neighbor tones: unease, corruption, horror, threat.
- Deceptive motion: uncertainty, ruined places, false safety.
- Clear cadence: village, victory, warmth, relief.

For loops:

- Avoid cadences that fully close too often unless the scene is safe or cheerful.
- Use a return gesture: held dominant-like tone, pickup, bass turn, filtered swell, or rhythmic fill.
- Change harmony under held notes to create surface continuity.
- Let bass and inner voices carry motion when melody is sparse.

## Bass And Low Register

Bass is a compositional role, not only low frequency.

- Pedal bass: anchors place and scale.
- Moving bass: creates travel and forward motion.
- Ostinato bass: creates pressure; vary it or it becomes mechanical filler.
- Contrary bass: makes melody feel argued against.
- Sparse bass: creates fragility or listening space.

Check: if the bass is muted, does the cue still have identity? If yes, bass supports. If no, the bass may be the whole composition; write a stronger motif or harmony.

## Counterpoint And Inner Motion

Use counterline only when it clarifies the scene.

Good counterline jobs:

- Answer the motif in a different register.
- Move while the lead holds.
- Foreshadow danger before the lead returns.
- Show environment vs player: place line against hero line.
- Create contrary motion to avoid blocky parallel writing.

Avoid:

- Constant doubling at the same rhythm.
- Random extra notes.
- Counterlines that fight SFX readability.
- Parallel layers with identical envelopes and no phrase relationship.

## Sustain And Voice-Leading Gate

Before rendering, write a 2-4 bar voice-leading sketch. It can be simple, but it must show how sound connects over time.

Pass conditions:

- At least one voice is tied or held across a barline or chord change.
- At least one common tone, suspension, anticipation, or delayed resolution is used intentionally.
- Bass, inner voice, melody, and counterline do not all change at the same instant for the whole phrase.
- Note lengths are varied by phrase role: pickup, statement, held pressure, answer, release, or return.

Rebuild if the sketch is only block chords, equal note lengths, parallel rhythm in every layer, or dry stops before each new harmony. This failure sounds like a chord chart being triggered, not music being performed.

## Rhythm And Meter

Rhythm is often the main scene identifier.

- Walking field: steady but not rigid; medium subdivisions, small pickups.
- Ruins/dungeon: slower pulse, uneven accents, sparse low motion.
- Haunted mansion: broken meter feel, hesitant gaps, small anticipations, long tails.
- Infernal field: half-time weight, ritual impacts, bass gravity.
- Desert travel: uneven 3+3+2 or hand-drum-like cycle, not only a shaker.
- Snowfield: slow long values, wide spacing, fragile high points, warm low entries.
- Boss battle: short collisions, phrase interruptions, harmonic acceleration, not default snare.

If the rhythm could fit any scene, it is too generic.

## Scene Recognizability Test

Before final output, answer without looking at the prompt title:

- What scene would a listener guess in 10 seconds?
- What musical evidence supports that guess?
- Which two wrong scenes could it be confused with?
- What must change to reduce that confusion?

If the answer is only "mysterious", "tense", "pretty", "sad", "hot", or "cold", the cue fails. The cue must imply place, action, and game function.

## Rebuild Triggers

Rebuild from the design card when:

- There is no motif or the motif is not repeated recognizably.
- There is no phrase form beyond a static bed.
- Harmony does not change, imply motion, or intentionally sustain a place.
- Bass has no defined function.
- The cue is identified only by timbre or sound effects.
- A listener could not distinguish the scene from a title screen, cave, generic horror, generic dungeon, or abstract ambience.

## Minimal Theory Pass For Procedural Rendering

When rendering with raw synthesis, do this before adding sound design:

1. Write a two-layer version: motif plus bass or harmony.
2. Make it work rhythmically and harmonically with plain tones.
3. Add one counterline or inner motion.
4. Add surface behavior: articulation, overlap, room, and dynamics.
5. Add sound-design details last.

If the plain-tone version is not recognizable, the final rendered cue will not become recognizable by adding effects.

## Music-First Gate

Before rendering ambience, impacts, noise, Foley-like events, or special timbres, produce a compact music-only sketch:

- Lead: one motif with at least two recognizable returns.
- Bass or harmony: a support part that creates motion, suspension, or return.
- Phrase form: at least a 4-bar statement-answer-variation-return, or an 8-bar A/A'/B/return.
- Cadence/loop: a pickup, held tone, bass turn, or harmonic preparation back to bar 1.

Evaluate the sketch without sound effects:

- Can it be hummed, tapped, or notated?
- Does bar 2 answer bar 1?
- Does the middle phrase change something meaningful?
- Does the final phrase point back to the start?
- Would it still be music on a piano or simple square wave?

If not, stop. The output is currently sound design, not music.

For haunted or ambient scenes, "music" can be sparse, but it still needs phrase logic. Sparse music is not the same as random isolated events.
